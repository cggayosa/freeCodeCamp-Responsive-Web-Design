St. Thomas Aquinas

St. Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274), sketched portrait often used to represent his life and work.

St. Thomas Aquinas, the patron saint of the University of Santo Tomas, was born in 1225 in Roccasecca, located in present-day southern Italy. He came from a noble and influential family, closely connected to the German Emperor through the feudal system of the time. Known for his quiet nature and deep humility as a student, Aquinas devoted his life to the pursuit of truth and wisdom. As a theologian, he wrote extensively about the doctrines of the Catholic faith. His most significant work, the Summa Theologiae, is a comprehensive synthesis of Catholic theology, demonstrating how philosophy serves as a “handmaid of theology.”

Beyond his scholarly achievements, St. Thomas Aquinas left a lasting legacy that continues to shape the Church and the academic world today. His teachings emphasize the harmony between reason and faith, inspiring generations of theologians, philosophers, and students. At the University of Santo Tomas, his patronage symbolizes the pursuit of truth grounded in both intellectual rigor and spiritual devotion. His life and works remind us that genuine wisdom comes not only from knowledge but also from humility and a deep love for God.
